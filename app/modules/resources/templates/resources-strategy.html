Lean UX

We apply Lean UX principles to the way we think, act, collaborate and design our products to be loved by our clients and users.

“Life’s too short to build something nobody wants” — Ash Mauya

Lean UX

It’s the practice of bringing the true nature of product to light faster in a collaborative and cross functional way with increased focus on building a shared understanding of the actual product experience being designed.

For this to happen three things come together:

  • Design thinking
  • Lean
  • Agile

Design Thinking

  • Focuses on discovering customers
  • Validates that the problem we are trying to solve exists and the solutions are a market fit

“Design thinking is a human-centered approach to innovation that draws from the designer’s toolkit to integrate the needs of people, the possibilities of technology, and the requirements for business success.”
— Tim Brown, president and CEO, Ideo

Lean Startup

  • Defined by the “Build, Measure & Learn” cycle with critical emphasis on learning
  • Promotes an experimental mindset of building minimal viable products (MVP)
  • Make measurement against clearly defined Key Performance Indicators (KPI)
  • Learning to continue or to pivot deliver better product in every iteration

Agile

  • Software development on value
  • Deliver working software to customers quickly and to adjust regularly to new learning

Multi-track Development Process

Continuous Delivery Track

  • Starts with an early discovery to define the product challenge & create a shared client understanding
  • Short 4-6 week cycle of cross-functional team to elaborate each prioritized epics
  • Validate conceptual wireframes & prototypes with clients, users and stakeholders

Detailed Design Track

  • Designers you add & elaborate details to conceptual prototypes needed for development
  • Deliver design in a consumable format (HTML) reducing the need for heavy documented handoffs

Agile Delivery Track

  • Dedicated designers & UX developers participate in daily scrum activities
  • UX developers perform integration of their front end pieces with business logic & service layers

10 Question Product Opportunity Assessment

See also Assessing Product Opportunities.

  1. Exactly what problem will this solve? (value proposition)
  2. For whom do we solve that problem? (target market)
  3. How big is the opportunity? (market size)
  4. What alternatives are out there? (competitive landscape)
  5. Why are we best suited to pursue this? (our differentiator)
  6. Why now? (market window)
  7. How will we get this product to market? (go-to-market strategy)
  8. How will we measure success/make money from this product? (metrics/revenue strategy)
  9. What factors are critical to success? (solution requirements)

Pitney Bowes Client Engagement Toolkit

An “engineering-led” client engagement is one that is incorporated into the product development process, with the ultimate goal of helping your team create products that are better suited for client needs. Examples include an inception/discovery workshop, a current client practice benchmark, or obtaining client input into new product or feature concepts.

This toolkit provides worksheets and checklist in the following categories:

  • Prior to the engagement
  • During the engagement
  • After the engagement

See also Engaging with Clients Toolkit and Engagement Guidelines.

    Form Fields

    The form field is our most often used input, and thus has a range of states and uses that inform our users. It is our rule that the content within a field is the primary design focus, while the label is secondary.

    Form Field States

    Form fields have four primary states, each defined by its form and type coloring:

    • Default
    • Active
    • Disabled

    Which states a user sees depends on whether or not the field is enabled or disabled.

    Enabled

    Most form fields are enabled by default. An enabled form field’s label and in-field prompt text is shown in slightly darker (“active”) grey, and the field will accept user input. This input text provides feedback by further darkening in response to mouse click or keyboard input that makes the field active.

    Active (Focused)

    When the user places a cursor within the field, it is said to be "Active" or "Focused"

    Disabled

    A disabled form field shows a light grey label and input copy and will not accept user input.

    Form Field Colors

    We use colors on fields to communicate meaning:

    Red to alert users to an input issue

    Text Field Sizes

    We have three sizes of form fields: small, default and large. The default size should be used for fields in all instances except:

    • With 20+ fields; use only small fields (Example: full page of entry fields)
    • With nine or less fields; use large fields (Example: sign in screens)

    Optional Form Fields

    We help users recognize which form fields are optional in two ways:

    • The in-field prompt text will be labeled Optional, and will be a lighter gray. (When the field becomes active, its text will display as a darker gray.)
    • The label concludes with an asterisk

    Writing Form Field Labels

    When writing your labels and in-field prompt text, it’s important to stay consistent across your entire product. Here are some basic guidelines for how to write these critical pieces of user interface copy.

    Style

    • Use short, active words and phrases.
    • Structure labels to avoid ambiguity in language.
    • To avoid confusing users, use consistent terminology for a given concept (e.g., “Input Recipient’s Name” vs. “Input Addressee’s Name” vs. “Input Contact’s Name”).

    Capitalization

    • Form field labels use sentence capitalization, except when they include words or phrases that are acronyms or proper nouns (e.g., “RMA number,” “Cost Account,” “ZIP Code”).

    Punctuation

    • Form field labels don’t conclude with punctuation, such as periods, colons. ● Don’t phrase your form field labels as questions.
